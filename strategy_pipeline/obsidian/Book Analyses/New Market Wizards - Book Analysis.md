## Overview

While Book 1 established that trend following and risk management are universal, Book 2 deepens the picture with statistical arbitrage, momentum investing, volatility normalization, and swing trading systems. ## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. Risk Management Remains Universal
**Supporters:** Eckhardt, Trout, Basso, Driehaus, McKay, Lipschutz
> Every profitable trader in Book 2 emphasises position sizing and loss-cutting as non-negotiable. Eckhardt explicitly warns: "If you don't manage risk, it will manage you."

### 2. Trend Following Validated Again
**Supporters:** Eckhardt, Basso, Sperandeo, Driehaus
> Eckhardt provides the most rigorous defense of trend following as a long-term edge. Basso adds volatility normalization as a refinement.

### 3. Volatility Normalization
**Supporters:** Basso, Trout, Eckhardt
> Adjusting position size and stops based on current volatility. Basso's system explicitly divides risk by ATR equivalent. New theme not prominent in Book 1.

### 4. Statistical / Probabilistic Thinking
**Supporters:** Trout, Eckhardt, Blake, Faulkner
> Trout and Eckhardt both stress that trading is a probability game. Blake achieves 25 consecutive profitable months through pure statistical timing.

### 5. Momentum Beats Value in Trending Markets
**Supporters:** Driehaus, Sperandeo
> Driehaus directly contradicts "buy low, sell high" — instead advocates buying high and selling higher. Strong momentum signals outperform mean reversion in trending conditions.

### 6. Session and Timing Filters Matter
**Supporters:** McKay, Lipschutz, Raschke
> When you trade matters as much as what you trade. McKay's news reaction, Lipschutz's London/NY overlap, Raschke's opening range — all time-dependent.
