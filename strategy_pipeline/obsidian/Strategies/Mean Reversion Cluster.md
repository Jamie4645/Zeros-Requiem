## Overview

Mean reversion strategies exploit the tendency of cointegrated asset pairs to revert to a statistical equilibrium. Chan provides the most complete codification: Johansen cointegration for multi-leg spreads, Kalman filter for dynamic hedge ratios (Sharpe 2.4), Bollinger band entries for binary signals, and half-life calculation for adaptive lookbacks. Thorp pioneered statistical arbitrage at Princeton-Newport. Blake's mutual fund timing is a form of mean reversion exploiting non-random behavior in fund NAVs. All share the premise that price spreads between related assets oscillate around a stationary mean.

## Core Rules Consensus

1. **Test cointegration before trading.** Chan emphasizes Johansen/ADF testing is mandatory. Correlation alone is insufficient — the spread must be stationary.
2. **Use half-life to set lookback periods.** The half-life of mean reversion determines optimal lookback and holding period. Shorter half-life = more responsive signals.
3. **Dynamic hedge ratios outperform static.** Chan's Kalman filter (Sharpe 2.4) beats static OLS (Sharpe 1.4) on the same pair because the relationship evolves.
4. **Position sizing via Kelly criterion.** Chan advocates Kelly for leverage, but half-Kelly for safety (full Kelly has too much variance).
5. **Mean reversion dominates at short timeframes.** Chan resolves the MR vs trend debate: intraday to days favors mean reversion; weeks to months favors momentum.

## Key Differences

- **Chan offers three approaches:** linear Z-score scaling (simplest), Bollinger band binary (medium), Kalman filter adaptive (best performance)
- **Thorp's stat arb is more complex:** hundreds of pairs simultaneously with dynamic rebalancing
- **Blake exploits fund NAVs:** a unique form of mean reversion on fund switches rather than asset pairs
- **Entry signals differ:** Chan uses Z-score thresholds; Thorp uses complex multi-factor models
